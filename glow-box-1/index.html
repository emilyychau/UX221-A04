Group Maker Project (GMP) 1

GLOW Box 1.0

Objective

Our group followed the critical making process to achieve the goal of GMP1, to “hack” the concept of a box. We reimagined it as a multipurpose and fully functional light fixture, designed to address the need for affordable yet customizable pieces of room decor. It would help to support different aspects of students’ lives–including their mood, mental health, and productivity habits.

Project Topic

To “hack” the concept of a traditional cardboard box, reimagining its size, shape and configuration. We took into consideration the accessibility of the prototype and its construction using sustainably sourced (i.e. recycled, repurposed, already owned) materials.

Aims & Objectives

The aim was to create a box that provides storage, has re-usable writing surfaces, and incorporates ambient lighting.

Designing and Building a Prototype

Materials and Tools

The construction phase began with gathering our materials and the necessary tools. We collected most items from recycling and around our apartments, but we did resort to purchasing some brand new materials or second-hand (if possible).

Materials, from left to right: Cardboard box, fairy lights, masking tape, hot glue sticks, wooden coaster, plastic picture frames, cardboard tube, pillowcases, magnets, superglue.

Tools, from left to right: Drill and drill bits, hot glue gun, ruler, box cutter, scissors

Building the Prototype

The building phase was split into a few distinct tasks: creating the base, attaching the picture frames together, assembling the light fixture and wiring, and finally creating the magnetic attachments.

First, the shape net for the cardboard base was sketched out and then cut out. The creases were lightly folded to aid in the formation of the box. It was painted to resemble a wood grain pattern and then affixed together using hot glue. To create the mid-section, the edges of the picture frames were scored using an Exacto knife. They were then superglued together in an overlapping fashion, it resembled a rectangular prism with open faces on opposite ends.

Next, the cardboard tube was wrapped in fabric. The two strands of fairy lights were wrapped around the tube and were led back into the cardboard base. Each cluster of magnets were super glued together and allowed to fully dry. Then, they were affixed to the lid while the reciprocal ones were affixed to the top face of the plastic picture frames; this created the magnetic opening for the lid. A similar technique was used to affix the cardboard base to the bottom of the picture frames.

Reconfiguration and Conversation

During the “show and tell” presentation, our team shared some of the reflections on the prototype and a few ideas that we had. We over-reached with our scope outlined in the project brief, ultimately, we could not incorporate the real time clock module nor were we able to solder components together. For our next steps, we’d like to address the scope and technical limitations that we encountered and explore some possibilities for integration with Bluetooth and the Internet of Things.

This phase was quite brief amongst our team, time was a major constraint. Nonetheless, we had some time to reflect upon a few aspects that could be improved on. By exploring a greater diversity of materials and considering the sustainability and environmental impact of our current prototype, we could look for eco-friendlier adhesives and opt for the use of LEDs to light the GLOW box.

Challenges

What challenges did you and your group encounter?

Having to navigate the unexpectedly large and bulky footprint of the prototype was a challenge. The steps taken to assemble all the components of the device during the “making phase” resulted in minimal time or attention given to cable/wire management. We didn’t have the time to solder components together and thus, it also contributed to the device’s larger footprint. There were also limitations with our team’s technical knowledge and understanding how the electronic components work together. It resulted in a lot of time dedicated to troubleshooting and researching the necessary code in order to realize our vision.

What did you do differently to respond to these challenges?

In response to the many technical challenges encountered throughout the build process, the incorporation of some components were entirely scrapped (RTC module). Other components like the second Arduino and separate power bank were added to accommodate for the extra pins needed to connect all the components into the device’s circuit.

Design Process

Which aspects of the design process worked, and why?

In both projects, the brainstorming and sketching aspects set us up for success. The preliminary planning was thorough and allowed us to approach the prototype construction with an approach already in mind. Any improvisation or deviation from the sketches occurred because we needed to adapt to the characteristics of the materials. In constructing our first GLOW box, the majority of our prototype was built out of recycled materials, repurposed items or things we already owned.

Which aspects of the design process did not work, and why?

Time efficiency and the distribution of time dedicated to each of the phases of critical making was uneven and less than ideal. For both projects, it left the team with a short amount of time for the final stage of reconfiguration and conversation (in GMP1) and for the sharing stage (in GMP2). We could have benefited from a dedicated session to reflect and think critically about each prototype and the critical making process that accompanied it. It would have yielded more rich and engaging insights to share as part of our “show and tell” presentations.
