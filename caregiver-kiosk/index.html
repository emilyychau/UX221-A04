Caregiver-Kiosk

For the last 12 weeks, my team and I have been working on improving the caregiver experience at St Joseph’s Lifecare Centre in our UX200 Design Thinking course.

Our solution idea is the Caregiver Kiosk, an interactive kiosk centred around improving communication for caregivers. It aims to keep caregivers informed about their loved one’s care by providing them with communication and information resources that support their long-term care experience.

Discover

Client & Background

St. Joseph’s is a not-for-profit long-term care (LTC) home in Brantford. They provide care for those whose needs needs cannot be currently met in the community. People enter into LTC for a variety of reasons including old-age, disabilities, advanced health conditions, etc.

User Group and Objectives

Two primary user groups consisting of caregivers and family members were identified.

Caregivers

  • Visitors designated by resident
  • Support:
    • Cognitive, emotional, and physical support
    • Mobility, feeding, hygiene
  • Family, friends, other person of importance

Family

  • Related to the resident
  • Improve quality of life 
  • Also support residents

The objective of this design thinking project was to:

Find an innovative solution to enrich the experiences of caregivers and family members of residents at St. Joseph’s Lifecare Centre

Generative Research

A competitive analysis was conducted on the direct competitors of St. Joseph’s, including other not-for-profit LTC homes in the area. The analysis also included other industries in which the care of a loved one was relinquished into the hands of an institution. Several best practices, strengths, and opportunities for differentiation were identified from this analysis.

Develop

Based on the client’s feedback, we ultimately took a step back from the proposed hybrid solution of the Caregiver-Resident Board and revisited the ideation stage. The feedback and comments on the proposed solution provided a different perspective to our initial idea and, therefore, were not seen as a complete invalidation of the Caregiver-Resident Board. Ultimately, it was important that our revised solution should still be driven by the data collected from our user research with the caregivers and family members at St. Joseph’s.

Ideation

Developing the screens for the kiosk demonstrated the type of information that the caregiver would have which were featured in the storyboards. We decided to include screens and pages on the kiosk, such as:

  • Home screen for caregivers to sign in
  • Covid-19 Screener
  • Today’s Care Team
  • Care Plan
  • Care Map
  • Meals
  • Activities
  • FAQs

Low-Fidelity Testing

In order to express how the kiosk would be helpful to caregivers, our method of a low-fidelity prototype were storyboards with a variety of different paths that can be applied to realistic situations that caregivers would encounter. 

  1. Caregiver locating their loved one’s charts and accessing personal information and their care map. This storyboard directly provides information to the caregivers and displays their ability to access up-to-date pages regarding their loved ones status.
  2. Display of the staff presently on shift, a display of the schedule of activities that the St. Joseph’s residents are able to participate in, and lastly, a page for commonly/frequently asked questions (FAQ).
  3. Caregiver receiving a physical copy of the care plan. This example highlighted how caregivers can receive a physical copy of their loved one’s documentation in an easier and more intuitive manner.

Overall feedback towards a kiosk system 

  • Interest in having information about staff, healthcare roles, and contact numbers
  • Interest in a digital COVID-19 screener for speeding up the process
  • Suggestion toward having multiple kiosks
  • Suggestion toward having more information available out COVID-19 outbreaks, meal times, and contact calling hours
  • Suggestion toward the possibility of remote access to information
  • Comfort in using technology
  • Preference for a hybrid (paper and digital) communication method

Usability Testing

Taking account of the changes from the low-fidelity testing, the medium-fidelity testing included the kiosk interface that was presented in the form of an iPad. The usability testing included four tasks that tested to see if the caregivers were able to navigate the interface and to see what changes needed to be made. 

Task 1:  Imagine you wanted to complete the COVID-19 check-in using the kiosk; how would you complete this process?

Task 2: You need to add an updated version of your loved one’s chart information to a file folder at home; how can you get a hard copy of this information?

Task 3: You’ve noticed that the shower in your loved one’s room is not working properly. If you needed to get in contact with someone about this issue but did not know who, where would you look to find this information?

Task 4: You notice that your loved one is seeing an “Otolaryngologist,” but you do not know what this provider does; where can you find information about this?

Implementation

A three-phased approach is suggested to ease into the adoption of a functional kiosk.

Firstly, further research and development must be conducted regarding the kiosk and its functionality, including necessary fundraising activities, cost breakdown for implementation, and sourcing vendors.

Next up would include training staff members and volunteers on the use of the kiosk and preparing them to assist caregivers and family members. There must also be preparation for the pilot test of the kiosk, monitoring usage metrics, collecting feedback, and continuing close-partnership with designers during the initial roll-out phase.

Future Steps

Further opportunities include the implementation of multiple kiosks, mobile app and website integration, expansion to other St. Joseph’s sites.
