Somatic Design Project (SDP) 2

FusionDeck

Project Topic

To design a device that incorporates various functional electronic components, 3D-printed components and leverages connectivity to the IoT. Users’ interaction with the prototype would provide an engaging somato-sensory experience, leveraging how they engage with FusionDeck through their sense of touch, motion, sight and hearing.

Aims & Objectives

The aim of this project was to create a device that supports productivity and efficient workflows, while also functioning as a discrete fidget toy.

Incorporates somatic design principles
Connectable to the IoT
Supports productivity
Streamlines workflows
Customizable button shortcuts
Accessible and ergonomic

Critical Making Process

The steps in the design process followed the five iterative phases of critical making. It begins with the first phase of Inspiration.

Inspiration

Our inspiration for this prototype came from a combination of different mini-keyboard console devices currently on the market. These devices serve a variety of creative professionals or students by offering streamlining shortcuts that can suit their exact needs.

Our goal was to emulate the same functionalities but also to expand the possible user group, making the device fully customizable and applicable to anyone’s digital workflow. The incorporation of a fidget toy as part of the prototype build would be a unique addition to the device. This idea incorporated the concept of a desktop fidget map, combining the benefits of fidgeting with the functionality of a productivity device/tool that supports more efficient workflows. 

Understanding

We conducted a brief literature search to support and validate the relationship between fidgeting and its effect on concentration, productivity, and self-regulation (Karlesky and Isbister 7-8).

Other research helped to validate the use of joysticks as a more ergonomic alternative to computer mice, citing its mitigation of wrist strains traditionally associated with mouse usage (Cook et al. 354; Government of Canada 2-3).

During this phase, we also referenced code uploaded for the Arduino forums and various YouTube tutorials. These resources helped us to write the code for the Arduino Uno, selecting the correct drivers to install in order to control the joystick and any additional code that needed to be written in Python.

Ideation

During the ideation phase, our team sketched out some ideas regarding the look and build process of the prototype.

To help us visualize the layout of the different electronic components, we created circuit schematics in TinkerCAD.

Making

The building phase was split into some different tasks: 3D modeling the button caps, writing the necessary code to test the joystick and Arduino, preparing the cardboard box to be used as the device shell, attaching the different components to the breadboard (buttons, the LED, jumper wires), adapting a new textured design for the textured button caps, securing the different components in place and cutting out holes for the components to protrude, decoration and the final assembly of the FusionDeck device shell.

In TinkerCAD, 5 different button cap textures were 3D modelled and prepared for printing using the Maker Lab’s 3D printer. 

As previously mentioned in the understanding phase, we referenced some code uploaded to the Arduino forums, the Arduino Uno libraries and code from various YouTube tutorials. All these resources assisted in writing the code for the Arduino Uno, and identifying the correct drivers to install to control the joystick.

The tactile buttons and the LEDs were connected to the miniature (mini) breadboards. Using jumper wires, the joystick was connected to the Arduino Uno and the mini breadboards. Next up, holes were cut out from the top face of the FusionDeck box in order for the electronic components like the joystick, buttons and LEDs to protrude out.

When all the components were secured in the cardboard device shell, some decorations and illustrations were added to the outside of the FusionDeck device. This included the FusionDeck logo and the addition of Minecraft stickers to hide some imperfections and incongruence in the device.

Sharing

Our team shared some next steps and reflections during our “show and tell” presentation. We’d explore the possibility of the functional integration of Bluetooth and/or Wi-Fi modules. To improve the inclusivity of the FusionDeck, we aim to include other accessibility features into the device. For example, incorporating adjustable volume control and auditory feedback can help to audibly indicate when buttons are pressed. This can be implemented with electronic components like a passive buzzer or a programmable audio output device.
